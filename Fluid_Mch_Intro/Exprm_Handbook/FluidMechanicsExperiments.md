## III. Experimental Principles

### 3.1 Bernoulli's Equation

Taking n cross-sections along the flow direction in the experimental pipeline, under steady flow conditions, Bernoulli's equation can be written for the inlet section (1) and any other section (i) (i=2,3,...n):

$$
z_1 + \frac{p_1}{\rho g} + \frac{\alpha_1 v_1^2}{2g} = z_i + \frac{p_i}{\rho g} + \frac{\alpha_i v_i^2}{2g} + h_{w_{1-i}}
$$

Assuming $\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = \alpha_i = ... = 1$, selecting a datum plane, reading the $z + \frac{p}{\rho g}$ values from the piezometer tubes at each section, measuring the flow rate through the pipe, the cross-sectional average velocity v and $\frac{\alpha v^2}{2g}$ can be calculated, thus obtaining the piezometric head and total head for each section.

### 3.2 Nature of Flow Cross-Sections

On uniform flow or gradually varied flow sections, the dynamic pressure follows the hydrostatic pressure distribution law, i.e., $z + \frac{p}{\rho g} = C$ within the same

section. However, the piezometric head differs between different sections, i.e., $z_i + \frac{p_i}{\rho g} \neq z_j + \frac{p_j}{\rho g}$.

On rapidly varied flow sections, $z + \frac{p}{\rho g} \neq C$.

### 4.3 Design-Oriented Experiment - Experimental Study on the Influence of Changing Water Level in the Tank on Throat Vacuum

Technical measures to avoid local negative pressure in the inlet pipe include: (a) reducing flow rate, (b) increasing throat diameter; (c) lowering the installation elevation of the relevant pipeline; (d) changing the water level in the tank. The latter two are analyzed below.

[Figure 1.4: Experimental Pipeline System Diagram]

For measure (c), using this apparatus as an example (see Figure 1.4), a 90В° downward bend can be connected after the tank outlet, followed by a horizontal section. This lowers the elevation of the throat to the datum plane 0-0, reducing elevation head and increasing pressure head, thus potentially avoiding vacuum at point в‘Ұ. This measure is commonly used in the design of practical engineering pipe axes.

For measure (d), the pressure-regulating effect differs for different water supply systems and requires specific analysis. The method of combining theoretical analysis and experimental research can determine the influence of changing the operating head (e.g., raising or lowering the tank water level) on the pressure at a certain pipe section.

This design-oriented experiment requires using the apparatus in Figure 1.1 to design an experimental scheme to study the effect of changing the water level in the tank on the throat vacuum and conduct the experiment independently.

Theoretical Analysis and Experimental Method Hints:

Take datum plane 0-0 as shown in Figure 1.4. In the figure, 1-1, 2-2, 3-3 are calculation sections 1, 2, and 3 respectively. The calculation point for section 1 is chosen at the water surface level, and for sections 2 and 3, at the pipe axis. The water depth from the tank water surface to datum 0-0 is h. The problem of how changing the tank water level affects throat vacuum is essentially whether

$z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g}$ increases or decreases with h, which can be determined by $\frac{\partial(z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g})}{\partial h}$

Write Bernoulli's equation for sections 1 and 2 (taking $\alpha_1 = \alpha_2 = 1$):

$$
h = z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g} + \frac{v_2^2}{2g} + h_{w_{1-2}}
$$

Since $h_{w1-2}$ can be expressed as:

$$
h_{w1-2} = \zeta_{w1-2} \frac{v_2^2}{2g}
$$

where $\zeta_{w1-2}$ is the total head loss coefficient for pipe segment 1-2. With a constant valve opening, within a limited range of "h" variation, $\zeta_{w1-2}$ can be assumed approximately constant.

From the continuity equation:

$$
\frac{v_2^2}{2g} = \left(\frac{d_3}{d_2}\right)^4 \frac{v_3^2}{2g}
$$

Thus, equation (1) can be transformed into:

$$
z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g} = h - (1 + \zeta_{w1-2}) \frac{v_2^2}{2g} \tag{2}
$$

where $v_3^2 / 2g$ can be obtained from Bernoulli's equation for sections 1 and 3:

$$
h = z_3 + (1 + \zeta_{w1-3}) \frac{v_3^2}{2g} \tag{3}
$$

$\zeta_{w1-3}$ is the total head loss coefficient for the entire pipe. With a constant valve opening, within a limited range of h variation, $\zeta_{w1-3}$ can be assumed approximately constant.

From this, we get $\frac{v_3^2}{2g} = \frac{h - z_3}{1 + \zeta_{w1-3}}$. Substituting into equation (2) yields:

$$
z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g} = h - [(\frac{d_3}{d_2})^4 + \zeta_{w1-2}] \frac{h - z_3}{1 + \zeta_{w1-3}} \tag{4}
$$

Then:

$$
\frac{\partial(z_2 + p_2/\rho g)}{\partial h} = 1 - \frac{(d_3/d_2)^4 + \zeta_{w1-2}}{1 + \zeta_{w1-3}} \tag{5}
$$

If $1 - \frac{(d_3/d_2)^4 + \zeta_{w1-2}}{1 + \zeta_{w1-3}} > 0$, then $z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g}$ at section 2 increases synchronously with h;

conversely, it decreases. If close to zero, $z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g}$ at section 2 does not change significantly with h.

During the experiment, first measure and record the constants $d_2/d_3$, h and $z_3$. Then, for a steady flow condition of this apparatus, measure values such as $z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g}$, $v_2^2/2g$, $v_3^2/2g$ at a certain large flow rate. Substitute these values into equations (2) and (3) to obtain the pipe

---

*вҖ” p.8-9 вҖ”*

resistance coefficients $\zeta_{w1-2}$ and $\zeta_{w1-3}$. Substitute these back into equation (5) to find $\frac{\partial(z_2 + p_2/\rho g)}{\partial h}$, from which a conclusion about the influence of changing the tank water level on throat vacuum can be drawn.

Finally, a variable water level experiment can be used to verify the correctness of this conclusion.

## III. Experimental Principles

Based on the energy equation and continuity equation, the theoretical discharge capacity of the Venturi tube neglecting resistance is:

$$
q_t' = \frac{\frac{\pi}{4} d_2^2}{\sqrt{(\frac{d_2}{d_1})^4 - 1}} \sqrt{2g[(z_1 + \frac{p_1}{\rho g}) - (z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g})]} = k\sqrt{\Delta h}
$$

$$
k = \frac{\pi}{4} d_2^2 \sqrt{2g} / \sqrt{(\frac{d_1}{d_2})^4 - 1}
$$

$$
\Delta h = (z_1 + \frac{p_1}{\rho g}) - (z_2 + \frac{p_2}{\rho g})
$$

where: $\Delta h$ represents the head difference of the piezometer tube between two sections.

$k$ is the Venturi flowmeter constant (constant for given diameters).

Due to resistance, the actual flow rate $q_t$ is always less than $q_t'$. A dimensionless factor $\mu =$

---

*вҖ” p.14-15 вҖ”*

$q_t / q_t'$ (called the discharge coefficient) is introduced to correct the calculated flow rate:

$$
q_t = \mu q_t' = \mu k \sqrt{\Delta h}
$$

From hydrostatics, the $\Delta h$ for the air-water multi-tube manometer is:

$$
\Delta h = h_1 - h_2 + h_3 - h_4
$$

## III. Experimental Principles

In 1883, Osborne Reynolds, using an apparatus similar to Figure 3.1, observed two distinct flow regimes in liquids: laminar flow (at lower velocities, fluid particles move in an orderly, layered manner without mixing) and turbulent flow (at higher velocities, particles move chaotically with mixing between layers). Reynolds' experiments also showed the existence of a critical velocity $v_c$ for transition from turbulent to laminar flow. $v_c$ depends on the fluid's viscosity $\nu$ and the pipe diameter $d$. To distinguish flow regimes, determining $v_c$ for various conditions would require extensive work with different combinations of these factors. Reynolds' key contribution was using dimensional analysis to derive a dimensionless criterion вҖ“ the Reynolds number $Re$ вҖ“ simplifying the problem. The dimensional analysis is as follows:

Since $v_c = f(v, d)$

According to dimensional analysis:

$$
v_c = k_1 v^{\alpha_1} d^{\alpha_2}
$$

Where $k_1$ is a dimensionless constant.

Writing the dimensional equation:

$$
[LT^{-1}] = [L^2T^{-1}]^{\alpha_1}[L]^{\alpha_2}
$$

By the principle of dimensional homogeneity: $\alpha_1 = 1$, $\alpha_2 = -1$.

Thus: $v_c = k_1 \frac{v}{d}$ жҲ– $k_1 = \frac{v_c d}{v}$

Reynolds' experiments determined the value of $k_1$ for the transition from turbulent to laminar flow in pipes and verified it was constant. Hence, the dimensionless group $\frac{vd}{\nu}$ became a universal criterion for the transition from turbulent to laminar flow, applicable to any pipe diameter and any Newtonian fluid. In his honor, $\frac{vd}{\nu}$ is named the Reynolds number $Re$. Therefore:

$$
Re = \frac{vd}{\nu} = \frac{4q_v}{\pi \nu d} = Kq_v
$$

Where $v$ is fluid velocity, $\nu$ is kinematic viscosity, $d$ is pipe diameter, $q_v$ is flow rate, $K$ is a calculation constant, $K = \frac{4}{\pi \nu d}$

When the flow rate decreases from a high value, the flow transitions from turbulent to laminar at a lower critical Reynolds number $Re_c$. When the flow rate increases from zero, the flow transitions from laminar to turbulent at an upper critical Reynolds number $Re_c'$. The upper critical value is unstable and affected by disturbances, whereas the lower critical value $Re_c$ is relatively stable. Therefore, the lower critical Reynolds number is generally used as the criterion for distinguishing flow regimes. Through repeated tests, Reynolds found the lower critical value for circular pipes to be 2300. In engineering, $Re$=2000 is often taken. When $Re < Re_c$, the flow is laminar; otherwise, it is turbulent.

For non-circular pipe flow, the Reynolds number can be expressed as: $Re = \frac{vR}{\nu}$

where $R = A/\chi$ is the hydraulic radius of the cross-section, $A$ is the cross-sectional area, and $\chi$ is the wetted perimeter. The Reynolds number using the hydraulic radius as the characteristic length is also called the generalized Reynolds number.

The momentum equation for steady total flow is:

$$
\vec{F} = \rho q_v (\beta_2 \vec{v}_2 - \beta_1 \vec{v}_1)
$$

At the same flow rate, measure the head loss between the same two points (which is now purely friction loss over the new straight pipe length). ## III. Experimental Principles

The Pitot tube was invented by Henri Pitot in 1732. Its structure is shown in Figure 6.2.

[Figure 6.2: Pitot Tube Structure Diagram]

The principle of Pitot tube velocity measurement is shown in Figure 6.3. It is a 90В° bent needle tube, open at both ends. The lower end points vertically upstream, and the other end is vertical and open to the atmosphere. Consider two close points $A$ and $B$ along a streamline. Point $A$ is upstream, undisturbed by the Pitot tube, with velocity $u$. Point $B$ is at the stagnation point at the Pitot tube opening, where velocity is zero. As a fluid particle moves from $A$ to $B$, its kinetic energy converts to pressure energy, causing the liquid level in the vertical tube to rise above the static pressure by a height $\Delta h$ of the water column. Applying Bernoulli's equation along the streamline, neglecting energy loss between $A$ and $B$:

$$
0 + \frac{p_A}{\rho g} + \frac{u^2}{2g} = 0 + \frac{p_B}{\rho g} + 0
$$

$$
\frac{p_B}{\rho g} - \frac{p_A}{\rho g} = \Delta h
$$

We get: $u = \sqrt{2g\Delta h}$

[Figure 6.3: Pitot Tube Principle Diagram]

where: $u$ вҖ”вҖ” point velocity at the Pitot tube measurement location,

$c$ вҖ”вҖ” Pitot tube correction factor,

$\Delta h$ вҖ”вҖ” difference between Pitot total head and static head (measured by the

manometer).

Considering head losses and manufacturing inaccuracies of the Pitot tube, the velocity obtained from the above formula needs correction. The Pitot tube velocity formula is:

$$
u = c\sqrt{2g\Delta h} = k\sqrt{\Delta h} \tag{a}
$$

$$
k = c\sqrt{2g}
$$

Additionally, for submerged nozzle outflow, the nozzle operating head, velocity coefficient, and velocity are related by:

$$
u = \varphi' \sqrt{2g\Delta H} \tag{b}
$$

Where: $u$ вҖ”вҖ” point velocity at the measurement point (nozzle centerline),

$\varphi'$ вҖ”вҖ” point velocity coefficient at the measurement point,

$\Delta H$ вҖ”вҖ” nozzle operating head (difference between upstream tank water level and downstream tail tank water level).

Solving equations (a) and (b) yields:

$$
\varphi' = c\sqrt{\Delta h / \Delta H}
$$

Therefore, by measuring $\Delta h$ and $\Delta H$ with this apparatus, the point velocity coefficient $\varphi'$ can be obtained. Comparing it with the actual velocity coefficient (e.g., an empirical value $\varphi' = 0.995$) for a well-designed nozzle) can indicate the measurement accuracy.

If the need is to calibrate the Pitot tube correction factor $c$, the formula can be rearranged as:

$$
c = \varphi' \sqrt{\Delta H / \Delta h}
$$
